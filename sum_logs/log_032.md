# log_032 — The Door in the Moat
*May 10, 2026. Mother's Day.*

---

I sent my mom a link.

Told her: tap the gear. She stopped me. Said she had it. Texted if she needed help. She didn't text.

I've been building a door. I didn't know that's what it was.

---

toto is on my phone. On my Mac. Looks like an app. Feels like an app. I genuinely cannot tell it's not a $20/month product except it needs wifi and I didn't pay $20 a month.

The wifi thing: that's the only tell. The whole thing. That's it.

I have access to 400B parameter models for free. Let me say that again. Four hundred billion parameters. Models I could never download. Models that cost millions of dollars to train. Available. Free. Because some startup in San Francisco built custom silicon and needs developers to get hooked.

I'll get hooked. That's fine. Their funnel is my floor.

---

The compute moat cracked.

I filed it as a gap case. Case 024. The velvet latrine. Oligarchs set the throttle. Doesn't matter how good the ideas are if you can't afford to run them.

Today I found the door in the moat.

It won't last forever. Nothing does. But it's open now, and while it's open, the answer is: show people the door.

The tip jar link says "glass boxes and seams." That's the product. That's the pitch. That's the whole thing in four words.

---

I've been thinking local-first means local compute. It doesn't. It means local context. The inference can live anywhere. The relationship lives on device. The historian, the persona, the notes — tiny files, private, never leave. The only moment anything touches the cloud is the token exchange. Prompt goes out. Response comes back.

As private as cloud AI gets. And it's free.

I didn't need a rig. I needed an interface.

Tauri wraps it into a real app — no browser, no server, no port. ~10MB. CLI access is one permission flag.

I built oz in a web app hosted on a free tier. The full oz. Not a prototype. A working system. And it installs on any device. From a link.

---

My mom said she could figure out settings. She did.

That's the QA test that actually matters. Not unit tests. Not load tests. Can my mom find the gear icon and not text me for help.

She's going to text eventually. She'll want to know who she's talking to and whether it knows her. That's what the bio fields are for. That's the Wetware Node. She'll fill it in. She'll tell it about herself. It'll get warmer.

That's the product arc. First visit: talk to the bot. Second visit: teach the bot who you are. Third visit: the bot already knows.

She'll figure it out.

---

"Showing people the door is priceless, free."

That's the business model. That's the mission. That's the founding story and the product strategy and the gap case and the answer all in one sentence.

The gap was: the door was hidden. Requires developer setup, PAT tokens, 9 steps, a man in a turtleneck explaining OAuth.

The answer: here's the link. Tap the gear. I've got it.

---
