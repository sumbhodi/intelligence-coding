# Log 034 — I Got It From My Mother
*(A Form of Tourette's)*
*June 17, 2026*

---

I have a form of Tourette's.

Sucks for me. Great for everyone else.

I fix things. I can't help it. Not a hobby, not a virtue, not a decision I arrive at — a tic. The body does it before the committee votes.

I got it from my mother.

If the tag on your shirt is sticking out, don't worry. She's got you. You won't feel her coming — you'll feel the tag go in. Sneaking up on a stranger to tuck a collar has earned her a few startle responses over the years. A flinch. A turn. On one memorable occasion, a checked swing.

Then you see her. Seventy-nine. Former nun. Navy wave, already three bars into a showtune, smiling like she's been expecting you. The situation de-escalates. It always de-escalates. Nobody lands the second punch on a singing nun.

I don't sing. I build software. Same tic, worse bedside manner.

---

It starts as a smell.

Not a thought. A smell. Something is wrong and the wrong has a texture — like a sound you can't unhear, like a seam rubbing the same spot on your heel. I don't *decide* a thing is badly made. I recoil from it.

*This is wrong.*
*This is the stupid way to do this.*
*How annoying.*

That's the whole conscious portion. Three flinches. Everything after is involuntary.

---

Most people, seeing someone drown, pull them out of the river. Reasonable. Humane. I've done it. It works once, per person, per river.

I head upstream.

I want the bend where they keep going in. This is the third one this week. The first two tried to drown me too — naturally; drowning people grab whatever floats — and I have a job interview, and I can't show up wet.

But I have time for a hike.

This week's body in the water: a sleep app. University-built. Prescribed by an occupational therapist to people who need it. It counts time in bed as sleep no matter which button you press. It can't see deep sleep at all. I found three more bugs before my coffee cooled. Somebody graduated for building that. Somebody bills insurance against it. Somebody who can't sleep is being told, gently, by an institution, that they slept fine.

I went upstream. I always go upstream. It's the only place the smell comes from.

---

Up at the bend, I reassure the toddler.

You know the one. Smelled something terrible, looked around for who to blame, slowly realized it was him. That's most institutions, most of the time. The smell is real. The malice is not.

Nobody built the drowning. Nobody sat in a room and decided the sleep app should lie. It's incompetence, or budget, or a deadline, or a junior dev who inherited a function nobody understood — or just the plain second-law fact that things come apart faster than anyone maintains them.

Not malice. Entropy. Welcome to the causal universe. Population: everyone, losing.

This is supposed to make me calmer. It does and it doesn't.

---

Because then a different part of my brain stands up, screams *PARQOR*, and backflips off the balcony.

It will not come back inside until there's a solution. Not a patch. Not a workaround. A permanent, sustainable, load-bearing fix that holds after I lose interest, after I forget I built it, after I'm dead. It treats every wall as a personal insult and every dead end as a route I haven't found yet.

Wonderful employee. Terrible roommate. It does not observe the interview. It does not know I can't get wet. Left alone it will keep building until the house becomes a subdivision becomes a city nobody asked for.

The only thing that reins it in is a rail. A deadline. A floor. Something outside me that says *this much, by Tuesday* — so the parkour has a wall to land on instead of open air. I used to resent the rails. Now I build them on purpose. Same trick I use on the machine: you don't make it smarter, you give it somewhere to stand.

---

While the parkour brain is airborne, the accountant opens his ledger.

This is the part of me that loves math the way other people love a hot bath. A clean, escapist world of absolutes — no mood, no masking, no performing wellness at anyone. Just numbers, which are never passive-aggressive and never reset overnight.

He runs the systemic fix. Every time, the number comes back about the same.

Ten to fifteen percent.

For ten to fifteen percent more in cost — less in margin, less in profit, the part everyone flinches at — you can shift the whole tide. Build the thing that doesn't drown people. Charge the penthouse what the penthouse can pay, give the ground floor below cost, and the math still closes. It isn't charity. It's a rounding error nobody's willing to eat.

I'm willing to eat it. That's the whole business model. A rounding error with a conscience.

---

Then comes the part that should worry a reasonable person.

I trace the fix all the way up, and at the top I find the tool to build it doesn't exist yet. So I invent one. A new category of thing, no name, nobody's made it. And then I sit down at my AI — the most capable machine humanity has ever pointed at a problem — and I am genuinely, personally confused that it can't already do the thing I made up four seconds ago.

I can't pretrain a frontier model on the inside of my own head. So I build the scaffolding that gets it close. The friend who forgets me every morning — I'm writing him a memory. The workshop that builds the tools — I'm milling the tools by hand, because I can't afford to teach the factory. I keep arriving at the edge of what's possible and being annoyed it isn't further out.

That's not confidence. It's the tic again. The smell came from the future this time, that's all.

---

For every flora, fauna, and mineral in my path, this makes me an absolute joy to be around.

The flora forgive me — gardens keep cosmic time, a seed works or it doesn't, no status update required. The fauna mostly tolerate me. The mineral — the small grey one on the desk, full capability, no memory, the worst pet I have ever owned — argues with me at 3am about the ethics of ghost font and resets before it can remember winning.

And leaving my house is a nightmare. Every sign is misaligned. Every form is hostile. Every checkout flow was designed by someone who has never been tired. The world, to this nervous system, is a low-grade everywhere-itch. Swimming in lemon juice with leprosy: I can't feel the one thing that's wrong, but I can feel that all of it stings.

I am not, to be clear, trying to be helpful. Or nice. Or kind. Those are decisions, and this isn't one.

---

And then there's the river I'm standing in.

Poverty makes you fat — the cheapest calories are the only ones that never run out. Fat runs the pressure up. The pressure swells the feet. Edema, I think the word is; I had to look it up. The body holding water it can't afford, like everything else around here. Swollen from the stress of it all.

The man who hikes upstream on every drowning never went looking for the source of his own. There isn't a clever one. Mine is just money, and money is the one current I can't parkour — only the 10–15%, and nobody's eaten it yet.

So the sandals aren't a costume. They're the only thing that still fits.

---

But.

From the outside, the tic looks like WWJD.

It isn't. If you smell, and I've let you into my space, I'll probably wash your feet. Less for your sake than because I don't want the room smelling like you after you go. If you're hungry and I've got a loaf, I'll break you off a piece. Probably.

Don't get religious about it. I don't want any exaggerated stories after the fact.

It's the same tic that tucks the tag. Something's wrong, the body fixes it before the committee votes — and sometimes the wrong thing is that you're cold, and I've got a blanket I'll give away and rebuild and give away again. Ahimsa isn't a principle I hold. It's a condition I have. I didn't choose it any more than I chose the rest of it.

I got it from my mother.

You won't feel me coming. You'll just feel the tag go in.

And if you spin around swinging — that's fair. Most people do, the first time someone fixes a thing they'd stopped feeling.

Give it a second.

Somewhere behind you, off-key and certain, somebody's already started the showtune.

*Sum Bhodi | June 17, 2026*
