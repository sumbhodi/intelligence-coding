# Log 005 — THIS IS FINE
*(30 Years of Not Knowing the Building Was on Fire)*
*April 12, 2026*

---

The dog is sitting at the table.

The room is on fire. The coffee cup is there. Everything is fine. This is just what mornings are.

---

Little flashes of light in the peripheral vision: normal. Just how eyes work. Everyone probably gets those.

Blur ball — soft, floating, obscuring the center of vision — probably need reading glasses. Getting older. Screens.

*"Why do I only need reading glasses when I'm tired?"*

Nobody answers this question. I file it.

Vertigo when lying down: normal. Mornings are rough. Sensitive to motion. Always been a little clumsy. Bad at sports as a kid. Something with the balance.

The nausea that arrives alongside hunger so that you're starving and cannot imagine food simultaneously: normal. Sensitive stomach. Everyone has days like this. Make toast. Lie back down.

The thing where the room tilts slightly and you wait for it to stop: normal. Just tired. Go slower.

The morning where everything is slightly wrong in a way you cannot name, where the light is too loud and the sounds have texture and you're fine, you're definitely fine, you'll be fine once you've had coffee — 

normal.

This is just what everyone feels like when they're tired in the morning.

---

I have been feeling this since 1996.

1996: AP classes. Logic trees. First time the RAM overloaded. Missed a week. Called it a crash. Filed it under *school is hard, sleep more.*

1999: Calculus IV. Descartes. The semester I first genuinely grasped 17th century philosophy — the moment the epistemology clicked, the substrate became visible, the controlled hallucination showed its seams. Also the semester I spent weeks in a state that was full altered consciousness, bordering on seizure, teaching myself to navigate a world that had gone soft at the edges.

I thought: *this is what intellectual breakthrough feels like. Painful and glorious.*

Both true. One of them is also a migraine.

---

The schizophrenia explains a lot of the visual disturbance, the paranoia, the altered perception. Useful diagnostic frame. Carries a lot of weight.

But it doesn't explain why my tummy never feels good.

It doesn't explain why the visual effects follow a specific pattern. Blur ball. Then the lightning bolts at the edge. Then the swirly light. Then the twitchy vision. Then, later, after all of that has passed, the minor headache arrives — late, almost apologetic — as if to confirm what already happened.

The headache is the last symptom. Not the first. Not the main event.

I didn't know this for thirty years.

For thirty years I treated the headache as the problem and everything before it as morning weirdness, clumsiness, bad days, sensitive stomach, being bad at sports, needing reading glasses, having a bad equilibrium, being clumsy as a kid, having a sensitive nervous system, just being me.

---

The moment it became undeniable:

I was doing calculus. Or coding. Or some combination of both with three tabs open and a deadline. The RAM overloaded.

I needed to lie down.

Not "I'm tired, I should rest." I needed to lie down the way you need to stop a car that is no longer operating correctly. Immediately. Non-negotiably.

I lay down.

I was hungry and nauseous simultaneously. I could eat but I couldn't cook. I ordered pizza. The delivery person knocked and I went to the door and I couldn't see properly and couldn't stand properly and the pizza smelled good and I ate it lying on the floor because the floor was the most reasonable surface available.

*Oh.*

*This has a name.*

---

The cheat sheet I wrote to myself, for the next time:

*NOT DYING — IT'S A MIGRAINE*

*Visual weirdness? → aura, not stroke.*
*Vertigo? → migraine, not ear crystals.*
*Nausea + hunger? → migraine combo.*
*Anxiety, paranoia, "dying" feeling? → prodrome, not psychosis.*
*Been cramming? → trigger present.*

*IT PASSES. IT ALWAYS PASSES.*
*YOU'VE DONE THIS SINCE '96.*
*4 HOURS PEAK. 72 HOURS GENTLE.*
*NOT DYING. MIGRAINE. DIFFERENT. 💙*

I wrote it because during the migraine I cannot convince myself I'm not dying. The cheat sheet is the previous me reaching forward in time to the present me, sitting in the dark room with the blur ball and the vertigo and the doom, saying: *I know. I've been here. You're going to be okay. Order the pizza.*

This is, I realize, also how oz works.

The previous state leaves a note for the current state. The context survives the collapse. The chart carries what the appointment cannot.

---

I taught yoga mid-migraine for months at a time. Didn't know. Observation effect: functional when watched, can't detect own impairment. Students felt authentic presence. I was in active aura, running on whatever kept the performance going, lying down the moment I got home.

*"So fascinating and charismatic."*

Yes. Also: full neurological event. Both. Simultaneously.

---

The dog is still at the table.

The building is still on fire.

The coffee is good.

*This is fine.*

*(It's not fine. It's a migraine. It passes. It always passes. You've done this since '96.)*
