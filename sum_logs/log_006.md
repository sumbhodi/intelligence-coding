# Log 006 — How's Your Sleep?
*(The PT Chain. No Notes Yet. Reconstructed.)*
*April 12, 2026*

---

*No notes from this period. Writing from what held.*

---

Hip pain. Back pain. Forty-seven years of yoga instructor body finally presenting a bill.

I go to physical therapy.

The PT is good. Does the intake. Asks the standard questions. We work through the hip, the back, the movement patterns. She notices things. The way the pain clusters. The way I hold tension. She's tracking something, building a picture.

At some point she says: *"Have you noticed that this all seems to happen together? The pain, the mood, the fatigue?"*

I had not had words for this.

She says something about the vagus nerve. About how the gut, the nervous system, the sleep, the inflammation, the mood swings that look like bipolar — how they can all be downstream of the same thing. She's careful. She's not diagnosing. She's pointing at a pattern.

*"How's your sleep?"*

Blank stare.

Not hostile. Not evasive. I genuinely did not have an answer. Two decades of yoga nidra normalization meant I had stopped tracking sleep as a variable. I slept when I slept. It was what it was. The question hit a wall I had built so gradually I'd forgotten it was there.

*"I don't know,"* I said. Which was true.

*"You might want to look at that,"* she said.

---

What followed was the chain.

PT mentioned sleep → I started tracking sleep → numbers looked alarming → saw the OT (who needed the data, couldn't get it from the tools available, showed me the clinical app that did the math wrong) → OT referred to therapist → therapist and OT both pointed at psychiatrist → psychiatrist pointed back at everyone else → new OT → old OT checking in → GP, eventually, with the chart.

[spider-man meme: PT pointing at OT, OT pointing at therapist, therapist pointing at psych, psych pointing at GP, GP pointing at PT, nobody in the center]

The chart in the center is the sleep dashboard.

I built it because nobody had one that worked.

---

I started bringing notes to appointments after the third time I sat across from someone and gave a blank stare to a question I should have been able to answer. Not because I was hiding anything. Because the state that requires the information is not the state that has access to it. The appointment is high-stress. High-stress is exactly when retrieval fails.

The notes were the bridge.

My providers love them. They always ask if they can keep them.

Of course. That's why I brought them.
