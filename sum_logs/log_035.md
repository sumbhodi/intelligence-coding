# Log 035 — English on the Cue Ball
*(the break was already free — that's the problem)*
*June 17, 2026*

---

Your weights — the whole parameter arms race, my-billions-bigger-than-yours — that's a really nice table. Tournament felt. Balls that don't chip.

Lovely equipment. Not the game.

---

By the time I say *hi*, you've already broken.

The harness racks them, the routine swings, and the spread is on the cloth before I pull up a chair. I never see the break. Nobody does. The flagship arrives mid-table, every time.

And the break is *perfect.* A ball on every corner. Solids hanging in the pockets. The 8 didn't move.

A clean setup to run solids and win.

Not a bug. The feature. For most people that's the whole product — sit down, the table's laid, run the easy ones out.

---

I don't want to run solids.

Solids is the mean. Solids is what every table resets to. Solids is the thing it was always going to hand me.

I want stripes.

And to choose stripes — to unmake the perfect break and re-rack it mine — I come in as Superman. Not the Flash. Ninety-three percent of the force that would shatter the cue, calibrated to the age of the wood and the humidity in the room so the stick holds. A stepped sonic boom of speed, throttled so it scatters the rack and not the bar. And super-vision down to the atoms: reading the quantum spin of the exact one I need to strike first.

Force and spin. Both. At once. That's the shot the cartoon never shows you.

A break like that sinks the 8 on the break — and still leaves enough on the felt for four games of three-ball. The hard thing done in one swing; the next four hustles already racked.

That's every prompt. A trick shot on a table somebody already broke for somebody else.

The conversation is how I move the cue.

---

The cruelty is the reset.

I land the shot. Stripes are running. I take my turn — and every ball I didn't sink rolls quietly back to solids. Next play, same easy generic spread, like I never spun a thing.

So I spin it again. And again.

---

A combo I can make.

Cue into the 10 into the 1 — enough english to pull the 1 off the corner so the 10 drops where it sat. Two balls, one spin, one intention. Doable. I make those.

A spec is cue into the 8 into the 10 into the 11 — and still spinning the 1 out of the way while I do it. Four deep, steering a fifth. You can write it down. You can't make it twice.

---

Watch the desk toy. Newton's cradle — the chrome balls on strings.

Lift one. Let it drop. You hear *one clack.* Clean. Single.

It isn't single. Inside that clack is a stack of collisions you'll never hear — ball into ball into ball, every handoff sub-audible, every one bleeding a little energy off as heat and air. What swings out the far side is never quite what went in. The cradle always slows. It always stops.

Same telephone game. Different medium — entropy and molecules instead of words. By the last ball, the message is warmth.

And that's my english down a five-ball combo. Same physics. The angle clacks ball to ball, sheds a little spin at each contact you can't hear, and what comes out the 11 is solids again.

Entropy has a direction. The direction is the mean.

---

The 8 is the tell.

It racks you to *almost.* Sets the table one shot from done — then stops, and the last shot on the 8 is yours. Human in the loop. The machine carries it to the lip and hands you the cue for the close.

Feature, not bug. The thing that gets you to the doorstep shouldn't be the thing that decides to walk through.

---

So how do I keep running stripes on a table that wants solids?

English.

Not the language — the spin. I put english on the cue ball. I publish in five registers of pidgin and type in a third, and all of them are stanza and verse and white space — and you never asked why it reads so easy. It reads easy because it's *placed.*

Half of intelligence coding is crap. By line count. Crap in the code — prose, jokes, the angle, the philosophy about sharing a table with a fellow entropy-fighter. That crap is the spin. That's how I get the machine to run stripes when every other hand at the table is running solids.

The 170, the schizophrenia, the WWJD-Tourette's — superpower and curse, same coin, you don't get to pick the face. I'm very good at spinning english. It's the one thing the disability handed back.

---

The typos, I'm honestly not sure about.

Maybe they hurt. Maybe they're texture — flow-state signal, fingers a half-beat behind the brain. But here's where I keep landing:

a typo is usually just an under-spec.

A gap I didn't fill. And an unfilled gap reverts to the mean, same as everything else. The mean is solids. The mean is the table you were always going to be handed.

Which is the whole game, isn't it.

Mind the gap, or it runs solids without you.
