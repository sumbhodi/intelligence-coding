# Log 003 — Hold My Root Beer
*(Parkour Through the Formulary)*
*April 12, 2026*

---

Cold season. Sinuses. Sudafed.

Standard over-the-counter stuff. Behind the pharmacy counter now because of the meth-lab tracking system — you sign for it, they log it, someone somewhere is watching the pseudoephedrine supply chain. Fine. I sign. I go home. I take it.

An hour later the dishes are done.

Not "I forced myself through the dishes." Not "I negotiated with my executive dysfunction for forty-five minutes and eventually completed the dishes." The dishes needed doing. I noticed. I did them. The signal fired. The task ran. Done.

*Oh.*

I sit with this for a minute.

*This is what it's like. This is what everyone else is doing. They just... start things. There's a signal. It fires. They go.*

I have been white-knuckling task initiation for forty years. I have been treating the absence of the signal as a character flaw, a motivation problem, a discipline issue. It is not. It is a missing neurotransmitter response that pseudoephedrine, a mild stimulant, just provided.

The math from here is not complicated.

Sudafed = pseudoephedrine. Ritalin = methylphenidate. Same mechanism. Different molecule. Different schedule. One is a controlled substance that requires a psychiatrist willing to prescribe stimulants to someone with a schizophrenia flag, a BP flag, an obesity flag, and a meth history from twenty years ago. The other is behind the pharmacy counter, tracked for meth labs, available to anyone who signs the clipboard.

I cannot get the first one. For the second one — the BP and the obesity numbers aren't good enough for any provider to sign off on extended use.

So I do what I do: I look for the route around the wall.

Research. What's mild, legal, similar mechanism, grows in the ground?

Sassafras. Safrole. Mild MDMA precursor, mild stimulant effect, historical root beer flavoring before the FDA banned it for liver concerns in rats at doses no human would ever consume. Not scheduled. Legal to possess. Grows wild in North America.

*I'll make root beer.*

Not metaphor. Not a bit. Actual root beer. One ounce of root bark to two gallons. Bottle in 500ml portions. Half a bottle when I need to start something and the signal won't fire. Sometimes one morning dose, one afternoon. Then weeks off. Don't really need it that often.

It works.

I am genuinely good at making root beer now.

---

A few years in, I decide to try the system again.

Specific goals. Written down. Reasonable:

One: establish a working relationship with a provider so that if I'm ever hospitalized again, there's someone who knows my chart, knows my history, knows to try Atavan before antipsychotics. We know what antipsychotics do. We have data. We'd like not to repeat the experiment.

Two: document the Sudafed response. Bring the evidence. See if there's a path to something prescribed, tracked, supervised. Move from DIY to sanctioned. I am not asking for anything exotic. I am asking for grade-school doses of immediate-release methylphenidate, maybe twice a day on work days, with monitoring.

Three: the BP. I know it's a concern. I can lose sixty pounds if that's what it takes. Tell me the number. Give me a target. I will hit it.

I am trying to integrate. I am bringing my homework.

---

She reviews the chart. Antidepressants: declined. Antipsychotics: declined.

*"If you're not going to take the antidepressant or the antipsychotic, I don't know why you're here."*

I understand where she's standing. Her toolkit has two items that apply to my chart. I won't take either. From inside her framework, she has nothing left to offer. She is not wrong. She is sincerely trying to help with the tools she has.

The tools don't fit. This is not her fault.

The tools were built for a patient whose diagnoses respond to the formulary. Mine do not, or the formulary does not respond to mine — the direction of failure is debatable. What is not debatable: antidepressants are the wrong mechanism (not serotonin, never was), and antipsychotics produced the only genuine delusion of my entire hospitalization stay within forty-eight hours of administration.

I have brought this data. It is in the folder. She has read it.

The stimulant request: schizophrenia diagnosis is flag one. BP is flag two. Obesity is flag three. Meth history, twenty years ago, is flag four. She doesn't finish reviewing the flags before the answer is no. I understand. Each flag individually is a reasonable concern. Together they produce a wall.

*"Will you at least write a Sudafed script so we can track my use and have data?"*

No.

Liability. Protocol. Insurance.

---

Here is the thing about the system: it is not malicious.

It is Vogon.

It has forms. The forms have boxes. The boxes were designed by people whose patients respond to the first or second medication tried. My boxes do not produce a valid output. No output is generated. The form returns an error. The form does not know it has returned an error. The form thinks it has been completed correctly.

This is the Vogon democracy of psychiatric care: not evil, just structurally incapable of processing an input that doesn't match the schema. The bureaucracy functions perfectly. The patient remains untreated. Both things are true. Nobody is to blame. The wall stands.

I see the wall.

---

Freestyle walking fool.

*PARKOUR.*

The root beer is parkour. Making my own ADHD medication from root bark in my kitchen is parkour. The ADB pipeline I built to pull my own health data from my own watch is parkour. The sleep dashboard I built because the clinical tool was doing the math wrong is parkour. Twenty years of zero hospitalizations on a protocol no provider will endorse is parkour.

Every wall the system produces, I find the gap. Not because I'm reckless. Because I've been reading the substrate since I was ten and I know where the load-bearing parts are and where the gap is just a gap.

The stimulant works. I know this. The system cannot formally acknowledge this. These two facts can coexist. I document the first one myself. The second one I wait on patiently, without particular resentment. The school nurse might say yes. The next provider might be different. The BP might come down. The world changes.

In the meantime: root beer.

Empirical record: twenty years, zero hospitalizations, functional protocol, nobody hurt, path still holds.

The system wanted to treat me with the tools that didn't fit.

I went around.

*Hold my root beer.*
