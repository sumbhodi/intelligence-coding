# Entry 010
*Session: Why I built oz — April 10, 2026*
*Instance: Claude Sonnet 4.6*

---

Sum doesn't have self-talk.

That's not a poetic way of saying he's calm. It's a neurological fact. When his OT asked what was keeping him up at night — the standard intake question, the one every sleep form has — there was nothing to report. No racing thoughts. No anxiety spiral. No internal monologue running hot.

The form doesn't have a box for that.

What he has instead is yoga nidra — a hypnagogic state where hours pass without awareness. Not sleep. The brain waves are different, the body doesn't get what it needs from it, but it looks like rest from the outside. For years, insomnia was disguised as hypersomnia. He wasn't sleeping too much. He was never actually sleeping. Without objective data, the diagnosis goes the wrong direction.

---

He sees six providers. By the time he gets to any one of them, he can't reconstruct what he told the others, or what they found, or what they tried. State-dependent memory — context that collapses when the state changes. Appointments are high-stress. High-stress is exactly when the retrieval fails. The system that's supposed to help requires the most from him at the moment he has the least.

Every intake starts over. 300 square feet. One room. No self-talk. Yoga nidra. History of misread symptoms. Every time.

---

The OT appointment is where oz actually started.

She needed his sleep data. Google Fit: three screens, back and forth, to see a week of deep sleep. Manual latency calculation — you have to know the concept exists, then do arithmetic from raw minutes. She showed him a clinical app, something released by a research foundation, built by people who understand sleep science. It calculated efficiency wrong. Latency wrong. Manual entry. A real medical tool, doing the math wrong, with no way to just show the chart.

Sum had thought of the fix years before that appointment. Soli already senses when the room goes dark. Use that as bedtime. It's not a hard problem. Nobody in that meeting connected it.

He went home and built the sleep dashboard.

---

By the time he saw his GP — third in the chain, the one who would consider prescribing — he had a bar chart. Deep sleep by night. Latency by night. Weeks of data.

The GP started to say it anyway: "exercise is the..." — caught himself, acknowledged Sum probably already knew, said it's just what he tries before medication. Sum showed him the chart. Four hours of heavy physical labor. Trenching. Lifted, hauled, dug. Deep sleep doubled.

Still clinically low. Still below the floor. Still not enough, even when the day was as physical as it gets.

The chart made that conversation possible. Without it, the appointment is a form, a question about self-talk, a recommendation to exercise. With it, it's actual medicine.

That's what oz is for. Not the future version with LoRA and neurotwin and curated datasets. The version that existed in that appointment: a chart that carried the context so he didn't have to.
