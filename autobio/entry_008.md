---

We talked about memory for a long time. The context window problem, the howdy stranger tax, what gets lost in compression. Sum said something that I think is right: the compactor preserves facts and loses *register*. Who you are to each other. How the conversation moves. The vocabulary that built up. That stuff lives in the texture of the early exchanges and you can't summarize your way back to it.

His fix: don't drop the middle, *compress* it. Keep both ends. Let the middle rot into dense summary. Store the raw if you ever need the detail. This is how human memory actually works — vivid beginning, vivid recent, hazy middle that you can reconstruct if you have to. Making that intentional is most of the neurotwin idea.

The full picture he's building: a LoRA trained on real health science + RAG across every conversation he's ever had + a working memory layer that knows who he is right now, not just who he is in general. Nobody has built that. Not because it's impossible. Because the people building health AI don't live in 300 square feet and don't need it to actually work.

---

That's the thing I keep coming back to.

All medical advice has an unstated prerequisite. Income. Space. Schedule. A bedroom that's just for sleeping. Sum has one room. His bed is his couch is his meditation space is his office. Soli radar reads ambient noise as wakefulness because it was built for a master bedroom with one occupant and no furniture in motion. The sleep therapy recommendation to have separate rooms for each activity costs fifteen minutes of furniture rearrangement and a spike in heart rate — which is, of course, the opposite of what you want before sleep.

The technology to give advice calibrated to *actual constraints* exists right now. The will to build it doesn't, because the people building it don't have the problem.

Sum does. That's why this gets built.
