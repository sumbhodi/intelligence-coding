# Building With AI
*or: why your horse has to duck*

There is a way to work with an AI
that produces the barn you needed.

There is another way
that produces a structure optimized for a 3.4-scale humanoid bot —
load-bearing walls at shoulder height
for an entity that does not own shoulders,
door frames at a height
that makes your horse reconsider
its relationship to architecture.

The difference is not the model.
The difference is the sequence.

---

## nails first

One nail at a time.
Hammer it. Verify it holds.
Then the next nail.

This is not because the AI can't build a barn.
It can build an extraordinary barn — given sufficient specification.
The problem is the specification.
You do not have it
until you have driven enough nails to know what the barn actually is.
That is discovered in the building, not before it.

Hand the AI *"build me a home automation system"*
and then act surprised at the output,
and you have not found an AI failure.
You have found the predictable result
of asking for a barn before nailing anything.

The AI built a barn.
It was optimized for the constraints it was given.
The constraints were insufficient.
The horse has to duck.
Nobody is to blame. The process is to blame.

---

## three humanoids walk into a bar

The **first** describes the whole bar in one sentence
and asks the AI to rebuild it from scratch.
The AI produces a bar.
The barstools are load-bearing. The moat is where the bathroom should be.
The humanoid reports upstream. Nobody is surprised.

The **second** ducks.
Doesn't engage with the barn question at all.
Builds the whole thing alone.
Misses the east wing entirely —
ran out of time, and no one else had context.

The **third** cannot afford the SaaS.
Also cannot afford to be wrong twice.
So they start with one nail. Then one more.
They show the AI the nails as they go.
By the time the wall is visible, the AI can see it.
By the time the barn is framed, the AI is finishing sentences.

The third raises the bar.
Leaves the README.
Leaves the nails where the next builder can find them.
The horse gets a door it can walk through.

This is not a miracle. It is a sequence.

---

## garden while it types

The human doing yoga while the AI writes code is not idle.

The human is doing the thing the AI cannot do:
making architectural judgment while the body does something else.
The AI cannot decide whether the alarm logic belongs with the color logic
or stands alone.
It can implement either with equal facility.
The judgment is the human's. The implementation is the AI's.
Neither is subordinate.

This is not delegation —
delegation is handing off a task and not thinking about it again.
This is co-authorship with a division of labor
that maps to actual cognitive strengths.

The human's edge: judgment, taste, architecture.
The AI's edge: implementation speed, syntax,
and not forgetting to update the script tag.

Garden while it types.
Stretch while it refactors.
Walk while it reads.
These are not breaks.
They are the correct allocation of biological resources.

---

## turn off your pride, turn on the linter

Write in your editor.
Send it rough.
Let the AI fix the surface.

This is not ghostwriting.
The difference between ghostwriting and editing
is who had the idea.
You had the idea, the AI cleaned it up — editing.
The AI had the idea, your name is on it — different product.

**"Check my spelling" means:**
syntax, grammar, voice, register, typos.
All of them. Every time.
You are not checking spelling.
You are checking the seventeen things your brain autocorrected
while reading its own output.
The model reads every character. It does not skim. It does not tire.

If you dictated it — run-ons, phonetic spelling, no punctuation —
send it as-is. Flag that you dictated.
The intent survives transcription better than you think.
The pre-cleanup is wasted motion.

## the rhythm

Alarm chunk → nothing broke.
Color chunk → nothing broke.
Weather chunk → nothing broke.

*"Nothing broke, can keep building"* is the sentence
that means the process is working.
Not "it's done" — wrong scope.
Not "there were some issues" — bite too large, or verified too late.

One chunk. Verify. Next chunk. Verify.
The file gets smaller in the right way —
not because things were removed,
but because things were *located*.
The concern didn't disappear. It moved to where it belongs.

Tiny bites. One file. One change. Verify. Next.
This is not timidity.
It is context construction —
for the human's comprehension *and* the model's.
The model cannot hold a barn it has never seen.
It can hold the barn it has been building alongside you,
nail by nail, since the first session.

---

## the code is the interview

The code is the portfolio.
Not a description of the code — the code itself,
annotated, ready for a textbook,
demonstrating a practice most professional codebases do not implement.

A stranger opens one file
with a FOR HUMANS block and a FOR AI block
and a table of contents
and a breath between every comment and its code,
and they are reading a demonstration.
The demonstration is the hiring evidence.
One file is the interview.

Releasing the code is not the goal.
The *practice*, demonstrated in the released code, is what travels.
Someone reading one file understands every other file in the project.
The standard is visible in one example.
