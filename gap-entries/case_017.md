# Gap Case 017 — Sleep Misdiagnosis / Provider Knowledge Gap

*Filed: 2026-04-12 | Taxonomy: Bear Country | Filed by: Sum Bhodi + Claude Sonnet 4.6*

---

## One Line

Insomnia since age 10. Misdiagnosed as hypersomnia. Forty providers later, nobody thought to ask if the sensor was accurate.

---

## The History

Sum has had insomnia since age 10. Not sleep hygiene insomnia. Not stress insomnia. Structural.

The body runs on a different clock. Natural rhythm: 10–12 hours in a world designed for 6–8.
In his 20s he had a working theory: alien from a planet with 36-hour days.
Not self-deprecating. Observational. The phase length fit.

For years this read as hypersomnia — the tendency to sleep long when given the chance.
Misdiagnosed for a decade. The presentation confused the clinicians.
Both labels were wrong. Neither mapped to what was actually happening.

**The yoga nidra pivot (20s):**
Found yoga nidra. Something shifted.
The key insight, stated precisely: *no more suffering ≠ getting more sleep.*
Yoga nidra is not sleep. It is rest at a depth that interrupts the suffering loop.
He did not sleep more. He stopped catastrophizing about not sleeping.
Normalized for two decades. Off the clinical radar. Functioning.

---

## The Gap Activates

Physical therapist asked about sleep during a routine session.
Standard intake question. Blank stare in response.
He had no language for it. Two decades of normalization = no active monitoring = no data = no story.

Started using Google Fit / Samsung Health to get data.
Immediately catastrophized.

This is the Bear Country trap at full deployment:
The tool gave him numbers.
The numbers looked bad.
The tool had no context for "always looked like this, always been fine."
The tool had no context for what his actual baseline is.
The numbers surfaced → anxiety → the sleep got worse → numbers got worse → spiral.

---

## The Provider Chain

Currently seeing: therapist, psychiatrist, physical therapist, occupational therapist, GP.
Fifth medication combination is being tried.
Latest rec: exercise.

First question Sum asks every provider: *"Is this lidar accurate?"*
Referring to: Soli radar in the Pixel, used by Google Fit for sleep staging.
Every provider: blank stare.

*"They did not teach that in medical school."*

This is not a failure of the individual provider.
This is the academic velocity gap (see case 018 for the structural version):
Consumer wearable iteration: 18-month cycles.
Clinical curriculum update: 5–10 year cycles.
The provider's training predates the sensor.
They cannot evaluate the data you bring because they weren't taught to evaluate it.

---

## The Soli Problem

Soli radar (Google Fit) overstages awake time when you're still.
Meditation. Lying in bed with eyes open. Yoga nidra.
Sum does all three. His data looks pathological by Fit's model.

First question to every provider → blank stare.
That blank stare is the whole gap.

The sensor categorizes "lying still while awake" as sleep stage data.
The provider reads the output without knowing it's built on a lidar model.
Treatment decisions get made on hallucinated data.
*"I am not crazy. Soli made it out to be worse than it was."*

The fix: watch staging first (wrist optical HR, more accurate for his pattern).
The home sleep study: needed to calibrate against clinical polysomnography.
Outcome: "worth treatment" — the underlying condition is real, the magnitude was inflated.

---

## The Sequence That Shouldn't Exist

*"I need a sleep study, at home, to see if my lidar is accurate.*
*Try this antidepressant.*
*Why don't I buy a watch first?"*

The watch came last. The antidepressant came first.
Home sleep study: not prescribed. In-lab study (3-6x the cost): the default.
Reason: guidelines written before home studies existed. Insurance coverage locked in for in-lab.

The cheap accurate answer was available. The expensive legacy answer was prescribed.
The patient suggested the watch.

---

## Bear Country Diagnosis

No malice. No negligence.

- The PT asked about sleep because it matters. Good intent. No training on sensor accuracy.
- Fit surfaced data because data is useful. No training data for "this user is a 36-hour person."
- Every provider made evidence-based recommendations based on available data.
- The data was partially hallucinated by a lidar that couldn't model Sum's physiology.
- Nobody in the chain asked "is this sensor accurate?" because nobody was taught to ask.

The gap is epistemological, not motivational.
Bear Country at every node. The aggregate is a treatment path built on a model the patient knew was wrong and couldn't get anyone to verify.

---

## The oz Angle

The dashboard that made the conversation possible:
- Sleep stages visible, not just a number
- Watch data primary — wrist optical, not room-level radar
- Source badge: W vs F. Patient can point to it. "This one is the lidar."
- Latency anchored to lights-off, not bed entry
- Extreme nights flagged, not just broken

The chart Sum brings to the appointment is now a chart providers can interrogate.
Not "your sleep efficiency is 58%."
"Your watch says 91% on nights you wear it. Fit says 77% on the same nights. Here's which sensor is which."

That's a different conversation.
