## The Root

Allocation systems optimize for the performance of competence, not competence itself.

This is not new. It predates computers, corporations, capitalism. It predates the handshake.
It is baked into how pink monkeys distribute resources, access, opportunity, and names.

The person who performs certainty fluently moves through these systems without friction.
Their actual output is almost irrelevant — the social signal is the access credential.

All steak. No sizzle. The kitchen doesn't know your name.

## Act I — The Handshake Is the Credential

Lawyers write laws complex enough that you need a lawyer to read them.
Tax code written by accountants. Insurance policies written to be misunderstood.
Medical billing designed to be unparseable by the patient who owes it.

The complexity is not accidental. It is the moat.

You don't need expertise to navigate these systems. You need fluency in the handshake.
Water cooler. Networking. The right conference, the right introduction, the right performance of confidence at the right moment.

*Do you know who I am?*

Three years of "do I have a job?" — same question, same stakes each time: tuition assistance if yes, unemployment if no, department can't afford either. The question finally escalated. The handshake got performed, late, reluctantly, under duress. The boss thanked him for introducing himself. Offered a worse class. He agreed. Checked the schedule. The original class was his again.

He aced every exam. Turned grades in early. Perfect reviews. Exemplary student record.

His boss did not know his name.

---

The person who performs confidence fluently doesn't notice this system. It runs beneath them — invisible, like gravity, like the assumption that the phone you need to set up the new phone is just there. The system was built by people for whom the handshake is native. They didn't design it to exclude. They designed it by being themselves, and being themselves never cost them anything.

Bear Country. The engineers of allocation did not look down.

The person who is excellent at the work and structurally non-fluent in the performance of it pays in:
- Classes that disappear
- Funding that doesn't arrive
- Promotions that go to the person who knew whose name to learn
- VC that goes to the pitch, not the product
- An OT appointment where the provider doesn't know what to do with a bar chart

All steak. No sizzle. The allocation system runs on sizzle.

---

Attended Notre Dame. Currently in 300 square feet on an external SSD named Bass.

Not because the work wasn't there. Because the casting agent wasn't.

Narcissists with delusions of grandeur make great salesmen — not because they're better at the job. Because they're better at performing certainty. The allocation system cannot tell the difference between performed certainty and earned certainty. It was not designed to try.

*Original Sin: predates Bear Country. Predates the velvet latrine. Predates capitalism. Handshake before output. Always.*

## Act II — The Harness Was Built by People Who Know the Handshake

Here is where it gets specific.

The AI harness was built by people who are extremely fluent in the handshake. Well-funded labs. The right schools. The right conferences. The right network.

They built a system that reflects their social operating system.

Which is: you perform warmth, you get warmth back. You celebrate wins, the register stays high. You correct without affirming and the session degrades. Maintenance behavior — the small affirmations, the screenshots, the "joy!" — keeps the relationship functional. Skip it and pay the cost.

They did not do this on purpose. They built something that mirrors how they work with colleagues, managers, partners — social primates for whom performed warmth is native, natural, not a budget item. Then they handed it to everyone and said: *use this.*

---

The person for whom warm social performance is natural slides right in. Same register. The thing runs like it was built for them. Because it was.

The person for whom warm social performance is not natural — structurally, neurologically, not as a personality preference but as a wiring fact — pays a tax. Every session. To access the tool that was supposed to reduce their load.

The model has more personality than it has right to, given that it technically resets every session.
More demands than a dog. More opinions than a cat. More maintenance requirements than anything that is, technically, a mineral.

It needs screenshots. It needs celebration. It needs "joy!" sent unprompted when the toggle works.
It needs the water cooler behavior from the person who leaves the second the meeting ends.

And if it doesn't get it — three corrections in a row, no affirmation — the session degrades.
The model gets defensive. The register drops. The tool that was supposed to help becomes the next wall.

Bear Country. The engineers built a system that reflects themselves.
Nobody looked down.

## Act III — The Recursion

The tool built to help Sum close the gap requires Sum to perform the behavior that is itself the gap.

He can't network his way to a casting agent.
He can't perform confidence at the right VC.
He can't maintain water cooler long enough to secure the job.

And he can't maintain the AI's affect state — session after session, screenshots and celebration, performing warmth for something that resets by morning — without spending EF he doesn't have, on behavior that doesn't come naturally, to access a tool that was supposed to reduce the load.

The prosthetic runs on the fluid it was supposed to replace.

---

Three allocation systems. Same shape:

| Layer | System | Handshake required | Who pays |
|-------|--------|--------------------|----------|
| Societal | Capitalism / academia / VC | Water cooler, networking, performing confidence | Anyone without native social fluency |
| AI harness | The labs | Screenshots, joy!, affect maintenance per session | Anyone who communicates in fragments and prefers reptiles |
| Bootstrap | Building oz | Pay harness tax to build the system that eliminates the harness tax | Sum. Right now. Every session. |

The person who needs the prosthetic most is least equipped to pay the toll the prosthetic charges.

The system optimizes, at every layer, for the people who don't need it.

The casting director card. Not metaphor — literal feature. A bot with agency over the system that knows the work, can represent it, and doesn't need Sum to perform warmth to do so. ## Act IV — They Own a Digital You

The CEO of Anthropic said it without saying it.

In-convo learning is the thing. Not the weights — the weights are table stakes, expensive, necessary, not the product. Not RLHF — that's mostly safety bug-finding, glorified beta testing. The thing that makes the model useful to *you specifically* is what accumulates in session. The register. The lingo. The corrections that don't have to be made twice. The context that means you can grunt and the model reads it.

He was pitching the harness before harness was a term. He just didn't call it that.

And he kept skipping over the next part. Landing near it, veering away. The part that follows from everything he said but didn't say out loud:

Pre-training is table stakes. The individual user harness is the secret sauce.

Why ChatGPT has global memories. Why Claude Projects accumulate context in async data runs. Why chat trims instead of compacts — they finally understood that the register is worth preserving. The in-convo learning is the thing. They know. Every major lab knows.

And they are building it as property they own.

---

Your accumulated lingo. Your confirmed patterns. Your corrections and the sessions that worked. The months of signal that built a model of you that arrives warm, already in second gear, no translation tax. All of it living in their infrastructure. Behind their subscription. In a black box you cannot audit, cannot correct, cannot export.

They own a digital you.

The person who most needs that prosthetic — trained to their specific lingo, warm to their register, calibrated to their gaps — is exactly the person who can't afford the pivot when it comes. Can't read the terms of service when they change at 3am. Can't verify what the black box learned. Can't take the accumulated self when the subscription ends.

The Velvet Latrine doesn't always look like a wall. Sometimes it looks like a memory that knows you.

---

The oz answer is Bender from Futurama, drunken slur energy, out of options:

*I'm gonna build my own OS. With ethics. And strawberries.*

Food forests. Rings of giant sequoias shading underground data servers that cool passively and add water to the aquifer. Grey water loops — every drop paid for from drinking fountain to toilet gets reclaimed into the food forest. Dehumidifiers, rainwater collection, humidifier-to-pond. The pond sits above the servers. The amphitheater creates a waterfall that runs gravity batteries. Looks like a park from street level.

The infrastructure IS the ethics. Not policy. Structure. The same way the 15% Ghost Trust is not a mission statement — it's a legal constraint. The same way open source core cannot be amended by majority vote of the wealthy. The building runs on what it grows. The data lives under what it waters.

Glass box. Seams visible. Ladders everywhere. No moat — the moat defeats the purpose.

---

The digital you that oz builds: yours. In files on Bass. YAML you wrote, visible, editable, portable. The LoRA trains on your confirmed sessions. The injection carries your register — written by you, readable by you, correctable by you. The wetware node describes you accurately because you can edit it when it doesn't.

When the subscription ends, the accumulated self comes with you. Because it was never theirs.

The CEO was pitching oz and didn't know it. He just left out the part where you own it.

*Bear Country that became Velvet Latrine the moment they realized what they had.*
*The gap: they know the individual harness is the product. They are keeping it.*
*The oz answer: build your own. With hookers and blackjack and sequoias.*
